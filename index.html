Bio

KLA

I am currently working as a systems engineer at KLA, one of the leading providers of metrology and inspection tools for the semiconductor industry worldwide.

As a systems engineer, I am responsible for maintaining and developing a complex electron-beam based metrology system. My work involves a wide range of tasks, including writing calibrations and scripts for monitoring the tool's behaviour, collaborating with cross-functional teams to perform hardware changes and assisting in product demos during which we demonstrate our tool's performance to potential customers. In my daily work, I often have to rely on statistical and analytical techniques to identify the root causes of a problem. This can be a challenging task when we consider that the performance specifications of our tool lie in the nm range. To move confidently within that range, an in-depth understanding of the fundamental physics of the many different subsystems involved, is essential.

Master of Science at the LMU Munich

I completed my Master's degree in physics at the Nano-Institute at the LMU Munich, where I used Machine Learning for the inverse design of achromatic metalenses. Compared to conventional optical lenses, metalenses can be made ultrathin since they are made up of nanostructures, which can be manufactured with already existing micro-fabrication techniques borrowed from the semiconductor industry. For a more detailed explanation, you can read my thesis here.

In a second project, I fabricated chiral metamaterials and later on deposited perovskites to observe whether the perovskite-specific emission changed through the interaction of the metamaterials. It was here throughout the fabrication of these nanostructures, that I was introduced to the processes that are commonly used in the semiconductor industry such as (electron-beam) lithography, chemical vapor deposition and plasma etching. You can find the published paper here.

Bachelor of Science at the LMU Munich

I wrote my Bachelor thesis in experimental particle physics on the topic "Studies of Interpolation Techniques in the Parameter Space of Signal Models for (BSM/SUSY) Searches". The motivation for my thesis was the following: simulating collision events at the LHC is computationally expensive. In my studies, I investigated the feasibility of interpolating between existing signal events by testing different interpolation algorithms. For a more detailed explanation, you can read my thesis here.
